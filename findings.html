What the data shows

Across Wisconsin's 72 counties, both income and education are strong, independent predictors of voter turnout. Counties where residents earn less and hold fewer college degrees consistently vote at lower rates — even in years with record-high statewide participation.

These communities aren't underrepresented because of voter suppression or apathy alone. Structural factors — access to time off work, civic infrastructure, educational resources — shape who participates in elections.

Why it matters for resource allocation

In a state decided by fewer than 21,000 votes in 2016, systematic underparticipation by lower-income and less-educated communities means the electorate that actually determines outcomes looks meaningfully different from the full population.

If resource allocation — schools, job training, civic programs — tracks with existing political power, the communities that need investment most may be least able to demand it. Turnout inequality isn't just a civic problem. It's a feedback loop.
