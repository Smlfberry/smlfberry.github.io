Is there a
representative electorate?.

Income and education play a large role in predicting who votes in the Midwest — and who doesn't. The communities left out aren't left out randomly. Here's what that means.

Explaining which communities get left out at the polls is not a straightforward task. While income and education appear to be powerful places to start, no simple relationship can explain who is most underrepresented in the electorate.

Finding 01

Income is a consistent predictor of turnout.

Across all 102 Illinois counties, wealthier counties vote at much higher rates. The gap between the highest and lowest income quartiles is roughly 7.5 percentage points — a structural pattern that holds across Illinois.

Finding 02

Education matters independently.

Counties flagged as low education only had the lowest average turnout of any group at 58.8% — lower even than counties with both low income and low education. Education is not just a proxy for income, but an independent driver of civic participation.

Finding 03

Low income and low education don't compound.

Counties with both factors averaged 62.6% turnout — higher than counties with low education or low income alone. This challenges the assumption that disadvantages stack to produce lower turnout.
